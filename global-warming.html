Global Warming

The long-term rise in Earth's average surface temperature, driven primarily by human emissions of greenhouse gases — and what we can do about it.

What Is Global Warming?

Global warming refers to the gradual increase in the average temperature of Earth's atmosphere and oceans since the late 19th century. It is the most prominent symptom of broader climate change, the term scientists use to describe the wider set of shifts in weather patterns, sea level, and ecosystems caused by a warming planet.

Main Causes

Human activity is the dominant cause of warming observed since the mid-20th century. The main contributors are:

  • Burning fossil fuels — coal, oil, and natural gas for electricity, transport, and industry release carbon dioxide (CO₂).
  • Deforestation — clearing forests removes carbon sinks and releases stored carbon back into the atmosphere.
  • Agriculture and livestock — produce methane (CH₄) and nitrous oxide (N₂O), both powerful greenhouse gases.
  • Industrial processes — cement production, refrigerants, and certain chemicals add long-lived warming gases.

Observed and Projected Effects

Climate

  • More frequent and intense heatwaves, droughts, and wildfires.
  • Heavier rainfall events and flooding in many regions.
  • Shifts in seasonal patterns and growing zones.

Oceans and Ice

  • Retreating glaciers and shrinking Arctic sea ice.
  • Ocean warming, acidification, and coral bleaching.
  • Rising sea levels threatening coastal communities.

People and Ecosystems

  • Food and water insecurity in vulnerable regions.
  • Displacement of communities from extreme weather and sea-level rise.
  • Loss of biodiversity as species struggle to adapt or migrate.
Why 1.5 °C matters: The IPCC finds that limiting warming to 1.5 °C above pre-industrial levels — rather than 2 °C — substantially reduces risks to ecosystems, food systems, and human health.

What We Can Do

Systemic Action

  • Transition electricity grids to wind, solar, hydro, and nuclear.
  • Electrify transport, heating, and industry.
  • Protect and restore forests, wetlands, and soils.
  • Price carbon and end fossil fuel subsidies.
  • Invest in research for storage, carbon removal, and sustainable agriculture.

Individual Action

  • Use energy-efficient appliances and insulate your home.
  • Walk, cycle, or use public transit; consider an electric vehicle.
  • Reduce food waste and eat more plant-based meals.
  • Support climate-aware policies, businesses, and candidates.
