The Science

How a Natural
Shield Became a Trap

Earth's atmosphere naturally traps some of the Sun's heat — a process called the greenhouse effect. Without it, our planet would be 33 °C colder and largely uninhabitable.

But since the Industrial Revolution we have pumped trillions of tonnes of CO₂, methane, and nitrous oxide into the atmosphere — thickening that blanket and trapping far more heat than the planet can handle.

The result: oceans absorbing more energy, ice retreating, weather systems loading up with extra heat and moisture, and an increasingly unstable climate.

Root Causes

What's Driving
the Crisis?

Since 1850, humans have dumped over 2.4 trillion tonnes of CO₂ into the atmosphere. Here's where it comes from.

⚡

Fossil Fuels — Energy & Transport

Burning coal, oil, and natural gas for electricity, heat, and transport is the single largest driver. Every litre of petrol releases ~2.3 kg of CO₂.

73%
🌳

Deforestation & Land Use

Forests absorb ~2.6 billion tonnes of CO₂ each year. Clearing them for agriculture and timber releases stored carbon while destroying our best carbon sink.

11%
🐄

Agriculture & Livestock

Cattle produce methane (80× more potent than CO₂ over 20 years). Paddy fields, synthetic fertilisers, and manure management all contribute heavily.

18%
🏭

Industry & Manufacturing

Cement, steel, and chemical production release CO₂ as an unavoidable by-product of the reactions involved — not just from burning fuel to power them.

5.2%

Consequences

The Damage Is
Already Happening

Global warming isn't a future scenario — it's reshaping life on Earth right now, in every country and every ecosystem.

🌪️

Extreme Weather

Hurricanes, wildfires, floods, and droughts are becoming more frequent and severe. A warmer atmosphere holds 7% more moisture per °C — meaning heavier rainfall and worse droughts.

🏔️

Melting Ice & Glaciers

Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets are losing ice 6× faster than in the 1990s. Mountain glaciers — freshwater sources for billions — are retreating rapidly.

🌊

Rising Sea Levels

Seas have risen ~20 cm since 1900 and are accelerating. Over 1 billion people live in low-lying coastal zones at growing risk of flooding and displacement.

🐠

Ocean Acidification

Oceans absorb ~30% of our CO₂ emissions. This acidification — already 26% more acidic than pre-industrial — bleaches coral reefs and disrupts marine food webs.

🌾

Food & Water Insecurity

Droughts, floods, and soil degradation are threatening crop yields. Climate change could push up to 216 million people into internal migration by 2050.

🦋

Mass Biodiversity Loss

Species are going extinct 1,000× faster than natural background rates. Habitat shifts and temperature stress are triggering what scientists call the sixth mass extinction.

Solutions

We Already Have
Everything We Need

The technology and knowledge to limit warming to 1.5 °C exists today. What's required is investment, political will, and urgency.

☀️

Renewable Energy

Solar and wind are now the cheapest electricity sources in history — cheaper than any fossil fuel. A full renewable grid with battery storage is technically and economically feasible now.

⚡ Highest impact
🌲

Protect & Restore Forests

Halting deforestation and restoring degraded land could deliver a third of the CO₂ reductions needed by 2030. It also protects biodiversity and water cycles.

🌍 Nature-based
🚗

Electrify Transport

Transport emits 16% of global emissions. Electric vehicles charged on renewable power eliminate tailpipe emissions entirely — and their lifetime cost is already lower than petrol cars.

🔋 Scaling fast
🏗️

Efficient Buildings

Buildings account for 40% of global energy use. Better insulation, heat pumps replacing boilers, and smart energy management can slash that while cutting energy bills.

🏠 Proven tech
🥗

Sustainable Food Systems

Shifting toward plant-rich diets, halving food waste, and regenerative farming could reduce food-system emissions by more than half — one of the biggest available levers.

🌱 Personal & systemic
📜

Policy & Carbon Pricing

Carbon taxes and standards that make pollution expensive create economy-wide incentives to decarbonise — proven to work in dozens of countries and sectors.

🗳️ Systemic change

The Clock Is Ticking

We have roughly a decade to halve global emissions and stay below 1.5 °C. Every fraction of a degree matters. Every year of delay locks in more warming. The cost of acting now is vastly lower than the cost of failing to act.
