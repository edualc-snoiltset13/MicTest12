Historic Climate Accord Signed by 142 Nations in Geneva

After two weeks of tense negotiations, delegates commit to binding emissions cuts and a $500 billion adaptation fund for developing economies.
By Elena Marchetti · Geneva · 6 hours ago
[ Delegates applauding in the conference hall ]

Negotiators from 142 countries concluded a marathon session early Sunday morning by signing what observers are calling the most ambitious climate agreement of the decade. The pact requires signatories to reduce greenhouse gas emissions by 55 percent from 2005 baselines no later than 2035, with intermediate verification checkpoints every two years.

The agreement also establishes a half-trillion-dollar adaptation fund administered jointly by the United Nations and the World Bank, with disbursement priorities favoring low-lying island nations and sub-Saharan agricultural economies. Critics have noted, however, that enforcement mechanisms remain voluntary, a concession demanded by several major industrial powers in the closing hours of the talks.

"This is the floor, not the ceiling," said UN Secretary-General Amara Okonkwo at the signing ceremony. "We have given ourselves a chance — now we must give ourselves the discipline."

Feature · Science

Inside the Mission to Bring Mars to Earth

By Dr. Patrick Owens · Houston

For six years, a tin canister no bigger than a coffee thermos has been making its way home. On Friday evening, the Endurance Sample Container docked with the SkyArc-7 orbiter in geostationary parking orbit, completing the most logistically demanding leg of an interplanetary relay that has involved three space agencies, fourteen launch windows, and an emergency software patch beamed from 220 million kilometers away.

The samples, drilled from Jezero Crater between 2021 and 2023, are expected to be released to receiving laboratories in late July following a quarantine protocol. Scientists hope the rocks will offer the first unambiguous evidence of whether microbial life ever took hold on the Red Planet — a question that has driven planetary science for more than half a century.

"What's coming back is small, but the questions it answers are enormous," said mission director Yelena Voss. "Every gram of regolith is a chapter in a story we've been trying to read since Viking."

Markets & Business

Tech Sell-Off Eases as Investors Eye Inflation Data

By Marcus Brennan · Wall Street Desk

U.S. equity markets closed mixed on Friday, with the S&P 500 edging up 0.4% and the tech-heavy Nasdaq Composite finishing flat after a turbulent week. Investors are weighing the latest core PCE figures, due Tuesday, against signals from the Federal Reserve that further rate cuts this year remain dependent on sustained disinflation.

Small-cap stocks fared better, with the Russell 2000 up 1.2%, reflecting a rotation into domestically focused names amid persistent uncertainty over global trade policy. Bond yields steadied, with the 10-year Treasury at 4.18%.

"There's a sense the worst of the volatility is behind us, but no one's calling the all-clear yet," said Helena Cruz, chief strategist at Meridian Capital. "Earnings season has been mostly reassuring, but the data prints in the next ten days will set the tone for the summer."

Commodities were mixed: gold reached $2,410 an ounce, copper held near $4.28 per pound, and wheat futures fell 1.8% on improved harvest forecasts from the Black Sea region.

Editorial

What the Geneva Accord Asks of Us

The Editorial Board

The agreement signed in Geneva this weekend will not, on its own, halt the changes already in motion. Nor will a treaty change the weather. But for the first time in nearly a decade, the world's largest economies have agreed not merely on a shared destination but on a shared timetable — and that is worth more than the headline number of any pledge.

"The work begins, as it always does, on Monday morning."

For the United States, the obligation is now domestic: to legislate, to invest, and to retire the carbon-intensive infrastructure that has defined our prosperity for a century. It will be expensive. It will be politically uncomfortable. It is also, finally, possible.

Universal Flu Vaccine Posts Strong Trial Results

By Dr. Mira Sato · Stockholm

A team led by researchers at the Karolinska Institute reported Saturday that their candidate universal influenza vaccine produced 87 percent protection across all major circulating strains in a Phase III trial involving more than 32,000 volunteers across nine countries.

If the results hold up under regulatory review — expected to begin in the United States, European Union, and Japan this summer — the vaccine could end the annual seasonal reformulation cycle that has defined influenza prevention since the 1940s.

"For the first time, we are not chasing the virus, we are anticipating it," said lead investigator Dr. Anders Lindqvist at a press briefing in Stockholm. Independent reviewers cautioned that long-term durability data remain limited.
