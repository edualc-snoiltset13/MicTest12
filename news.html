From Our Geneva Correspondent

Historic Climate Accord Signed By 142 Nations

After fourteen days of negotiation, delegates commit to binding emissions cuts and a half-trillion-dollar adaptation fund for vulnerable economies.
§   §   §
Delegates rise to applaud the closing signature, Palais des Nations

Negotiators from one hundred and forty-two countries concluded a marathon session early Sunday morning by signing what observers are calling the most ambitious climate agreement of the decade. The pact requires signatories to reduce greenhouse-gas emissions by fifty-five per cent from 2005 baselines no later than the year 2035, with intermediate verification checkpoints every two years.

The agreement also establishes a five-hundred-billion-dollar adaptation fund administered jointly by the United Nations and the World Bank, with disbursement priorities favouring low-lying island nations and sub-Saharan agricultural economies. Critics have noted, however, that enforcement mechanisms remain largely voluntary, a concession demanded by several major industrial powers in the closing hours of the talks.

“This is the floor, not the ceiling,” said United Nations Secretary-General Amara Okonkwo at the signing ceremony. “We have given ourselves a chance — now we must give ourselves the discipline.”

The signing was punctuated by an unscheduled address from a delegation of Pacific Island foreign ministers, who pressed for an earlier compliance deadline of 2032. Their amendment was tabled but is expected to return at the implementation conference in São Paulo this autumn.

Analysts at the International Energy Agency estimate that, if fully implemented, the accord would limit global mean warming to roughly 1.7 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels by mid-century — a figure that, while above the long-debated 1.5-degree threshold, would mark the first time the international community has formally aligned policy with a number on that side of two degrees.

Pacific Bloc Resumes Stalled Trade Talks

By Daniel Pham
Diplomatic motorcade arriving at the summit

The twelve-nation Pacific Trade Council reconvened on Saturday for the first time since negotiations stalled in February. Diplomats are expected to focus on digital-goods tariffs, port-of-call modernisation, and a new framework for semiconductor supply-chain transparency.

The talks are taking place under unusual public scrutiny, as several member states approach domestic elections in which trade policy has become a defining issue.

A Special Report

Inside The Mission To Bring Mars To Earth

Six years, three space agencies, and one tin canister no bigger than a coffee thermos.
By Dr. Patrick Owens · Special to the Chronicle · Houston

For six years, a small canister of Martian rock has been making its way home. On Friday evening, the Endurance Sample Container docked with the SkyArc-7 orbiter in geostationary parking orbit, completing the most logistically demanding leg of an interplanetary relay that has involved three space agencies, fourteen launch windows, and an emergency software patch beamed from two hundred and twenty million kilometres away.

The samples, drilled from Jezero Crater between 2021 and 2023, are expected to be released to receiving laboratories in late July following a quarantine protocol modelled on the one developed for the Apollo lunar returns.

Scientists hope the rocks will offer the first unambiguous evidence of whether microbial life ever took hold on the Red Planet — a question that has driven planetary science for more than half a century.

“What's coming back is small,” said mission director Yelena Voss, “but the questions it answers are enormous. Every gram of regolith is a chapter in a story we've been trying to read since Viking.”

The handover marks a rare moment of unalloyed scientific cooperation. NASA, the European Space Agency, and the Japanese Aerospace Exploration Agency have shared command of the mission since its 2019 charter — an arrangement that has survived three changes of administration in the United States and the wholesale restructuring of two partner programmes.

Markets & Business · Wall Street Desk

Tech Sell-Off Eases As Investors Watch Inflation Data

By Marcus Brennan

United States equity markets closed mixed on Friday, with the S&P 500 edging up four-tenths of a per cent and the Nasdaq Composite finishing flat after a turbulent week. Investors are weighing the latest core PCE figures, due Tuesday, against signals from the Federal Reserve that further rate cuts this year remain dependent on sustained disinflation.

Small-capitalisation stocks fared better, with the Russell 2000 up one-and-two-tenths per cent, reflecting a rotation into domestically focused names amid persistent uncertainty over global trade policy. Bond yields steadied, with the ten-year Treasury at 4.18 per cent.

“There's a sense the worst of the volatility is behind us, but no one is calling the all-clear yet,” said Helena Cruz, chief strategist at Meridian Capital. “Earnings season has been mostly reassuring, but the data prints in the next ten days will set the tone for the summer.”

Commodities were mixed: gold reached $2,410 an ounce, copper held near $4.28 a pound, and wheat futures fell one-and-eight-tenths per cent on improved harvest forecasts from the Black Sea region. The dollar index softened against a basket of major currencies, slipping for a third consecutive session.

Editorial · The Opinion Of The Chronicle

What The Geneva Accord Asks Of Us

The Editorial Board

The agreement signed in Geneva this weekend will not, on its own, halt the changes already in motion. Nor will a treaty change the weather. But for the first time in nearly a decade, the world's largest economies have agreed not merely on a shared destination but on a shared timetable — and that is worth more than the headline number of any single pledge.

“The work begins, as it always does, on Monday morning.”

For the United States, the obligation is now domestic: to legislate, to invest, and to retire the carbon-intensive infrastructure that has defined our prosperity for a century. It will be expensive. It will be politically uncomfortable. It is also, finally, possible.

Whether it actually happens depends less on the eloquence of diplomats abroad than on the discipline of legislators at home. A treaty is a promise; a budget is its proof. The Senate will receive an implementation package within the fortnight. The Chronicle urges its prompt and unsentimental consideration.

Health · Stockholm

Universal Flu Vaccine Posts Strong Trial Results

By Dr. Mira Sato

A team led by researchers at the Karolinska Institute reported on Saturday that their candidate universal influenza vaccine produced eighty-seven per cent protection across all major circulating strains in a Phase III trial involving more than thirty-two thousand volunteers across nine countries.

If the results hold up under regulatory review — expected to begin in the United States, the European Union, and Japan this summer — the vaccine could end the annual seasonal reformulation cycle that has defined influenza prevention since the nineteen-forties.

“For the first time, we are not chasing the virus, we are anticipating it,” said lead investigator Dr. Anders Lindqvist at a press briefing in Stockholm. Independent reviewers cautioned that long-term durability data remain limited and that real-world effectiveness can differ from controlled-trial figures.

A second trial, focused on adults over the age of seventy, is scheduled to enrol in October.
