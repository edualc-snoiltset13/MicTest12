# Solving Global Warming: A Structured Approach

## Framing

Global warming is fundamentally a **stock-and-flow problem**. Cumulative atmospheric CO₂ determines warming, so any solution must address two things:

1. **Flow** — drive annual greenhouse gas emissions to net zero.
2. **Stock** — remove some of what we've already emitted.

A useful evaluation lens for every proposed solution is:

- **$/ton CO₂e abated** — does it scale economically?
- **Gigaton potential** — does it move the needle? (Global emissions are ~50 GtCO₂e/year.)
- **Speed to deploy** — decades matter; we don't have unlimited time.
- **Political and social feasibility** — the best technology is useless if it can't be built.

No single lever closes the gap. The realistic path is a portfolio.

---

## 1. Decarbonize Electricity

Electricity is ~25% of global emissions and the foundation for decarbonizing everything else (transport, heating, industry).

- **Solar and wind** are now the cheapest new generation in most markets. Constraint is no longer cost — it's **transmission buildout, permitting, and storage**.
- **Grid-scale storage** (lithium for hours, pumped hydro and emerging long-duration tech like iron-air for days) is essential to handle intermittency.
- **Nuclear fission** provides firm, dense, low-carbon power. SMRs may lower capital risk, though deployment timelines remain unproven. Politically divisive but technically high-leverage.
- **Geothermal** (especially enhanced/EGS) is underrated as a firm clean baseload.
- **Fusion** is plausible but not on the critical path for the 2030s.

**Biggest near-term lever:** permitting reform and transmission. The U.S. and EU can both build enough clean power; they cannot currently *connect* it.

---

## 2. Electrify Everything Else

Once electricity is clean, electrification cascades decarbonization through the economy.

- **Transport** — EVs are now cost-competitive on TCO in most segments. Charging infrastructure and grid capacity are the real bottlenecks. Heavy trucking may need batteries *or* hydrogen depending on duty cycle.
- **Buildings** — heat pumps replace gas furnaces with 3–4x efficiency gain. Retrofits at scale need skilled labor and financing models.
- **Industrial heat** — most industrial heat is below 200°C and can be electrified directly with heat pumps or resistive heating.

---

## 3. Hard-to-Abate Sectors

These need dedicated solutions because electrification alone doesn't work:

- **Steel** — green hydrogen direct reduction (H₂-DRI) replacing coking coal. First commercial plants exist (HYBRIT, H2 Green Steel).
- **Cement** — process emissions from calcination (~60% of cement CO₂) require carbon capture, alternative binders (limestone calcined clay), or novel chemistries.
- **Aviation** — sustainable aviation fuels (SAF) for now; possibly hydrogen for short-haul long-term. Electrification is range-limited.
- **Shipping** — ammonia and methanol look most viable as zero-carbon fuels.
- **Agriculture** — methane from ruminants (feed additives like Bovaer, breeding), N₂O from fertilizer (precision application, nitrification inhibitors), and land use change.

These sectors will likely require **carbon pricing or mandates** to bridge cost gaps until clean alternatives reach parity.

---

## 4. Carbon Removal

Even with rapid decarbonization, IPCC pathways assume significant carbon removal. We need to scale it from ~megatons today to gigatons by 2050.

- **Nature-based** — reforestation, afforestation, mangrove restoration, soil carbon. Cheap per ton but limited by land and permanence (forests can burn).
- **Enhanced rock weathering** — spreading crushed basalt on cropland. Cheap, durable, co-benefits for soil. Verification is hard.
- **Direct air capture (DAC)** — engineering solution, durable storage, but currently ~$400–$600/ton. Needs to reach <$100 to scale.
- **Bioenergy with CCS (BECCS)** — durable but competes with food/land.
- **Ocean alkalinity enhancement** — large theoretical capacity, real ecological uncertainty.

**Important caveat:** carbon removal is *not* a substitute for emissions reductions. It's a complement for residual emissions and historical drawdown. Treating it as a substitute creates moral hazard.

---

## 5. Policy Instruments

Technology alone doesn't deploy at the pace needed. Policy choices shape what gets built.

- **Carbon pricing** (tax or cap-and-trade) — economically efficient but politically fragile.
- **Subsidies and tax credits** (IRA-style) — politically durable, less efficient, but proven at moving capital.
- **Performance standards** — vehicle efficiency, building codes, clean electricity standards. High certainty of outcomes.
- **Border carbon adjustments** (e.g. EU CBAM) — prevent leakage, create incentive for trade partners.
- **Permitting and transmission reform** — unglamorous but often the binding constraint.
- **Phase-out mandates** — ICE bans, coal retirements. Effective when paired with alternatives.

A pragmatic mix: **subsidies to scale tech, standards to lock in deployment, pricing where politically viable, permitting reform to unblock supply.**

---

## 6. Adaptation

Some warming is locked in. Adaptation is not optional — it's where most climate suffering will be averted or felt.

- Heat-resilient cities (cool roofs, urban greening, cooling centers)
- Sea-level rise responses (managed retreat, defenses, building code updates)
- Climate-resilient agriculture (drought-tolerant crops, irrigation, diversification)
- Public health systems prepared for vector-borne disease shifts and heat mortality
- Resilient infrastructure (grid hardening, water systems)

Adaptation is also a **climate justice** issue: the populations least responsible for emissions are often most exposed.

---

## 7. Geoengineering — Last-Resort Category

Solar radiation management (stratospheric aerosol injection, marine cloud brightening) could cool the planet quickly and cheaply but:

- Doesn't address ocean acidification.
- Creates a "termination shock" risk if stopped abruptly.
- Has uneven regional effects (potentially disrupting monsoons).
- Raises severe governance questions.

**Position:** research is justified to understand risks. Deployment should require global governance frameworks that do not yet exist. Geoengineering is not a substitute for decarbonization and treating it as one is dangerous.

---

## 8. Cross-Cutting Enablers

Often-overlooked foundations that determine whether any of the above actually happens:

- **Critical minerals supply** — lithium, copper, nickel, rare earths. Mining, refining, recycling.
- **Workforce** — millions of electricians, welders, HVAC techs needed.
- **Permitting reform** — applies to nearly everything physical.
- **Innovation funding** — public R&D for early-stage tech (DAC, fusion, green cement).
- **Just transition** — fossil fuel workers and communities need real economic alternatives, not slogans.
- **International finance** — developing countries need cheap capital for clean infrastructure.

---

## Honest Uncertainties

Areas where I think confident answers are unwarranted:

- **Climate sensitivity** at the tail — how bad could outcomes be at 3°C+?
- **Tipping points** — when do Amazon dieback, ice sheet collapse, or permafrost methane become irreversible?
- **DAC trajectory** — will it follow a solar-like cost curve or stall?
- **Public willingness** — will populations sustain political support across multi-decade transitions?
- **Geopolitical stability** — climate cooperation depends on it; climate change undermines it.

---

## Priorities If I Had to Pick

If forced to rank for **leverage × tractability** over the next decade:

1. **Build clean electricity faster** (permitting, transmission, firm clean power).
2. **Electrify transport and buildings** (EVs, heat pumps at scale).
3. **Industrial decarbonization** (green H₂, steel, cement) — needs policy push.
4. **Methane** — short-lived, high-impact, cheap to reduce.
5. **Scale carbon removal** to drive cost curves down before we need gigatons.
6. **Adaptation everywhere**, especially in the global south.
7. **R&D moonshots** (advanced geothermal, fusion, novel cement, ocean CDR).

---

## Closing Thought

Global warming is solvable with technologies and policies we already understand. The bottleneck is **deployment speed**, which is a function of politics, capital, and supply chains — not physics. The question is not whether we *can* solve it, but whether we will choose to move at the pace the problem demands.

The honest answer is: we are moving faster than five years ago, slower than the science requires, and the gap is closeable but only with sustained urgency.
